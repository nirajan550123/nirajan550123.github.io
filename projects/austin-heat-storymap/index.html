Interactive Story Map · Austin, Texas

The heat beneath the city.

On a summer afternoon, the ground in some Austin neighborhoods runs nearly 10°F hotter than in others just minutes away. This map shows where the city's surface burns hottest — and why the difference isn't random.

The values here are land surface temperature from satellite thermal imagery — the heat of roofs, roads, and ground, which on a summer afternoon runs far hotter than the air. That surface heat is what drives the urban heat island.

Six neighborhoods, one summer

Each neighborhood here is shaded by its average land surface temperature across three recent summers, measured from Landsat thermal imagery. Cooler blues, hotter reds.

02 · The hotspots

The east and core run hottest

The hottest neighborhoods sit in the urban core and east side — dense, paved, historically industrial. Their summer surface temperatures climb well past 115°F.

03 · The shade

Tree canopy is the clearest signal

Switch the map to tree-canopy cover and the pattern nearly inverts. Across all 65 Austin neighborhoods, more canopy means lower surface heat — a strong, consistent relationship. Leafy Barton Hills is the coolest neighborhood here, and the greenest.

r = −0.87canopy ↔ heat, citywide (n = 65)
04 · The pavement

And the pavement

Impervious surfaces — roofs, roads, parking lots — absorb and re-radiate heat. The most paved neighborhoods are the hottest. Less canopy and more concrete compound each other.

r = +0.83pavement ↔ heat, citywide (n = 65)
05 · Who feels it

Income matters — but less than the landscape

Heat in Austin is shaped first by the physical landscape. Income plays a real but weaker role: lower-income neighborhoods tend to run hotter (r = −0.51 citywide), though there are clear exceptions. Central East Austin is among the hottest in the city despite rising incomes from gentrification, while leafy, affluent Zilker stays cooler. The clearest lever for cooling a neighborhood isn't its wealth — it's its trees and its pavement.

Cooling a city starts with knowing where it burns.

This map turns satellite thermal data, tree-canopy and impervious-surface cover, and census income into one question a planner can act on: where would shade do the most good?

Land surface temperature: median of clear-sky Landsat 8 & 9 summer scenes (June–August, 2022–2024), Google Earth Engine. Tree canopy and impervious surface: NLCD / USFS, zonal means per neighborhood. Boundaries: City of Austin Neighborhood Planning Areas. Income: U.S. Census ACS 2018–2022 (B19013), area-weighted from tracts to neighborhoods via a PostGIS spatial join. Correlations computed across all 65 planning areas. Full methodology and code on GitHub.
