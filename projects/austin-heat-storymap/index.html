Interactive Story Map · Austin, Texas

The heat beneath the city.

On a summer afternoon, two Austin neighborhoods minutes apart can differ by more than 10°F. This map shows where the city runs hottest — and why the difference isn't random.

01 · The city

This is Austin in summer

Every neighborhood here was mapped for its average land surface temperature on a hot afternoon, using satellite thermal imagery. Cooler blues, hotter reds.

Pan and click any neighborhood at any point to see its numbers.

02 · The hotspots

The east and core run hottest

The hottest neighborhoods cluster in the urban core and east side — dense, paved, and historically industrial. Here, summer surface temperatures push well past 100°F.

03 · The shade

Now look at tree canopy

Switch the map to tree-canopy cover. The pattern almost inverts: the coolest neighborhoods are the leafiest, often in the hilly west. Shade is doing real work.

—canopy ↔ heat correlation
04 · The pavement

And the pavement

Impervious surfaces — roofs, roads, parking — absorb and re-radiate heat. The hottest neighborhoods are the most paved. Less canopy plus more concrete compounds the effect.

—impervious in hottest areas
05 · Who feels it

Heat isn't shared equally

Overlaying median income shows the hottest, least-shaded neighborhoods often have the lowest incomes — the communities least able to offset heat with cooling costs. Urban heat is an equity issue, not just a climate one.

Cooling a city starts with knowing where it burns.

This map turns satellite thermal data, tree-canopy cover, and demographic data into one question a planner can act on: where would shade do the most good?

Built with Leaflet and vanilla JavaScript. Land surface temperature derived from Landsat thermal bands; tree canopy and impervious surface from NLCD; neighborhood boundaries and income from the City of Austin open data portal and U.S. Census. Methodology and code on GitHub.
